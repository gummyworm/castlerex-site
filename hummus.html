Hummus חוּמוּס

Recipe

passive prep time: ~14-24 hrs
cook time: ~1-2 hrs
active prep time: 30 mins

equipment

  • large pot (to soak and cook chickpeas)
  • food processor or blender

ingredients

  • 1 lb (453g) dried Chickpeas
  • juice of 1 medium lemon
  • 1/4 to 1/2 bulb garlic (to taste)
  • 340g tahini (3/4 cup)
  • 1 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1 Tbsp cumin

chickpeas

  • Rinse chickpeas and remove debris, cover with 2-3" water
  • Soak chickpeas for 12-24 hours with 1 Tbsp baking soda
  • Change water, rinse chickpeas, and add to pot with 1/4 tsp baking soda
  • Bring chickpeas to slow boil skimming foam from pot until it is no longer produced
  • Skim skins from water as they rise (optional)
  • Boil chickpeas for 1-2 hours until easily mashed between fingers

hummus

  • Add tahini, garlic, lemon juice, salt, and cumin to food processor with enough cooking liquid to make pourable mixture
  • pour mixture into a container to be added while processing
  • process chickpeas, in batches, with a portion of the tahini mixture and more cooking water to obtain desired texture
  • combine batches into container and refridgerate. save cooking water to thin as needed as hummus thickens

to serve

  • Drizzle of olive oil
  • Spices to taste: salt, paprika, cumin, and/or za'tar

As far as food goes, hummus is a hilariously political topic. Perhaps because of its sudden worldwide "coolness", it seems every middle-eastern nation wants to make sure they get their due credit for their part in its creation. The contention is especially obvious if you dare credit Israel with the invention of the dish. Read the comment section for any video or recipe that contains the words hummus and Israel in any combination and you will most surely hear loud objection to the contrary. Obviously, hummus predates Israel by many years, but (politics aside) I do think Israel is a must-visit if you want to understand the artistry and refinement that can be applied to the craft of making hummus. In Israel, there is a hummus culture unlike anywhere in the world. Abu Hassan in Jaffa is widely regarded as the home of the best hummus the world has to offer. I tend to agree. Shlomo and Doron's is my personal favorite. And there are an exhausting number of other fantastic hummus places where you can get ethereal hummus.

It would be foolish and arrogant to say my own hummus is any match for Abu Hassan's, but I do consider myself quite adept when it comes to making the dish nonetheless.

When it comes to perfecting any craft, I do think it is important to expose yourself to examples of that craft executed at a masterful level. It is for this reason that I opened this recipe with a discussion of Israeli hummus. The first hummus I had during my visit to Israel, steps from my hostel, moments after my arrival, was so far beyond anything I'd yet tasted, and so far above my (already high) expectations. My very notion of "good" hummus was immediately altered. It reminded me of my first latte in a big city, where the barista actually bothered to create the latte art I'd thus far only seen in pictures. If you want to really dive down the hummus rabit hole, you owe it to yourself to visit Israel, Lebanon, or another middle-eastern nation with a strong hummus culture.

One final note before I proceed with the recipe: hummus really is pretty simple to make. I am a fanatic, but in all likelihood, you are not. I expend a shocking amount of mental energy on thoughts of hummus. Don't complicate things at the jeopardy of your own enjoyment of the process. It's actually very hard to make bad hummus. And you definitely don't need to go to Israel to up your game. But if you find yourself accumulating mounds of empty tahini jars, I do reccommend it. ;)

Now on to some things I've learned after a few hundred batches.

Chickpeas

The first step to a great batch of hummus are the chickpeas. Look, canned chickpeas are fine in a pinch. Any hummus is better than no hummus. The result of a batch made from a can will be far superior (and cheaper) than anything you'll find in the supermarket. But, for great hummus, there is no alternative: you need to start with dried chickpeas.

I've experimented with various sizes. Many sources will say that you should use the smallest chickpeas possible. This is true. You'll get noticeably smoother hummus from small chickpeas. I theorize that this may be more a result of the decreased cook time required, but I cannot say for sure.

The difference between small and large chickpeas is quite small. Where I live, there is thankfully an excellent middle eastern grocery that stocks the smaller variety of chickpea, but chances are you'll have to go with the so-called "Garbanzo" beans stocked at many supermarkets. Don't fret too much.

You'll need to soak the chickpeas before cooking them. Rinse the chickpease and add enough water to cover them with a couple inches. You've probably heard that is important to add baking soda to help soften the beans. Again, this is good wisdom. Add a good amount to the soaking water: I usually go with a full Tbsp.

I've had good success soaking anywhere from 8-24 hours. After soaking, rinse the beans again and add to a pot to cook. Cover the chickpeas again and add a little more baking soda (somewhere under 1 tsp). Too much baking soda in the cooking water will impact the taste of that water, which is not good since you'll want to use that water later on to thin the hummus to your desired cosistency.

The cook time varies quite a bit. You'll need to use your own judgment to determine when they're done, but expect anywhere from 30 minutes to 2 hours. If it is taking exceptionally long, it is probably due to a deficit of baking soda.

Ideally, the skins will detach after a short while of boiling at which point you should scoop them from the surface and discard them. This will make a HUGE difference in the texture of the hummus and, to some extent, the taste. I think hummus made without skins is truly indulgent, but you will get a substanitally bigger batch with, presumably, far greater fiber content if you leave the skins. And of course, it will also cost you far less effort to leave the membranes on the chickpeas. While you will rarely hear of someone seeking grit in their hummus, I actually sometimes leave the skins on to add a little texture.

When the chickpeas are done cooking, they should be almost mushy. If you cook them too long they will darken and your hummus will lose some visual appeal, but don't worry too much- it will still taste great. In fact, hummus from "overcooked" chickpeas reminds me of one of the greatest Hummusias I visited (repeatedly) in Tel Aviv: Abu Adham. If you're willing to accept the discoloration of a prolonged cooking process, another hack becomes available to you: the slow cooker. Put soaked chickpeas on high for basically as long as you want (4-10 hours) and you'll have a usable, albeit unphotogenic, mush.

Ingredients

I like to make a sauce from the remaining ingredients while the chickpeas cool. To do this I add the juice of 1 lemon, about half a bulb of garlic, 1 1/2 tsp of salt, 1 Tbsp of cumin, and 340g of tahini. Put all the ingredients for this sauce into a food processor and mix with enough cooking water to make a very light-colored and easily pourable liquid. You should be able to easily pour the resulting mixture from the food processor without needing to scrape.

This produces a sauce that is light on citrus, and heavy on garlic and tahini flavor. What I like about making this sauce separate is that you can really isolate the variables that you want to adjust when personalizing the hummus. For me, this means relatively little lemon. It's fine to start with any one recipe, but you WILL want to make adjustments to the ratios of these ingredients. You will probably want more lemon. Most hummus I've tried is a little citrusy for my taste, which must mean the common palette prefers it more than I.

Combining

Add some chickpeas to the food processor and pour in some of the tahini mixture> You'll most likely need to process the hummus in batches. I have a 14 cup food processor and it takes me several batches. If you skinned the chickpeas, it will probably take less batching due to the lower overall volume. I prefer to start with a relatively dry batch and make it thinner as I go. This makes the last batch easy to extract cleanly from the food processor (again with little, if any, need to scrape). Add more cooking water as you like to each batch to achieve the desired consistency. Process each batch longer than appears to be necessary. I like to run the processor for 5-7 minutes each batch.

The hummus will thicken considerably upon cooling, so it is wise to save the remaining cooking liquid for adjusting the texture later. You can cool the chickpeas to fridge temperature before processing, which will make the cooking water a gel like consistency. Doing so will give you a better idea what the final cooled product will be like. You can also speed up the cooling process by processing some ice with each batch of hummus.

Do not be discouraged at the amount of time you spend making hummus the first time. It is surprising how much more efficient you will get with each successive batch. :)

Storing and serving

Hummus needs to be refrigerated to prevent spoilage. I've never had issues keeping hummus for 5 days. More than that and you may notice some deteriation in flavor, and I wouldn't advise trying to keep it more than a week.

For some reason, you will find a lot of people online who claim some authority on the matter of hummus suggest that the only way to serve hummus is warm or room temperature. Those same people will tell you that it ought to be served immediately after making. In my experience, it really is best cooled for at least 12 hours for best flavor. This is true with almost all Levantine dips: baba ganouj, muhamara, etc. I do think it is worth trying "fresh", but the flavors are noticeably more subtle.

There are 3 main ways to customize your hummus. I've mentioned two: the tahini/citrus/garlic ratio and the consistency. The third is presentation. This is the most fun part of the process in my opinion. I like to make a couple extra cups of chickpeas to keep for garnishing. I make a small basin in the hummus and fill the indentation with a scoop of warm chickpeas. Around the chickpeas I drizzle some (extra virgin) olive oil and sprinkle the dish with cumin and Hungarian paprika (regular paprika is fine too, I just like the bright color of Hungarian). The way that every Hummusia in Israel presents their hummus is what defines them. At the end of the day, it is a very simple dish. To make a truly memorable hummus is an art that inspires all the senses including sight.

Some other additions to try serving on your hummus:

  • chopped flat leaf parsley
  • a sliced boiled egg
  • ful
  • tahini sauce (a mixture of water and tahini)
  • fried eggplant
  • sauteed mushrooms and onions
  • spicy tomato sauce (a la shakshuka)

In Israel, ful, tahini sauce, and chickpeas are the standard options while olive oil, parsley, and a generous array of spices are implied. You can go as far as you want when presenting. One of the best bowls of hummus I had in Tel Aviv (from a falafel joint called Mifgash Osher) was topped with fried cauliflower. Be as creative or traditional as you like.
